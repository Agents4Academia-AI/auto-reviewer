# Auto-Reviewer Agent Pipeline

## 0. Input Processing and Paper Parsing

**Goal:** Convert the paper into structured information.

**Inputs:**

* Paper PDF 
**Actions:**

* Extract title, abstract, introduction, related work, method, experiments, results, limitations, conclusion.
* Extract figures, tables, equations, algorithms, claims, and citations.
* Build a structured paper representation.

**Outputs:**

* Section-level summary
* List of main claims
* List of key technical contributions
* List of experimental results
* List of assumptions and limitations

---

## 1. Overall Understanding

**Goal:** Understand the paper at a high level before judging it.

**Questions:**

* What field or subfield is this paper in?
* What problem does it address?
* What is the research question?
* What are the main claims?
* What are the proposed contributions?
* What evidence does the paper provide?

**Outputs:**

* One-paragraph paper summary
* Problem statement
* Main contribution list
* Claim-evidence map

---

## 2. Section-Level Analysis

**Goal:** Analyze each section independently and identify potential issues.

**For each section:**

* Summarize the section.
* Identify key claims.
* Identify supporting evidence.
* Check whether the section is clear, complete, and internally consistent.
* Flag unclear definitions, missing assumptions, weak arguments, or unsupported statements.

**Sections to analyze:**

* Abstract
* Introduction
* Related Work
* Method
* Theory / Analysis
* Experiments
* Results
* Limitations
* Conclusion
* Supplementary Material, if available

**Outputs:**

* Section-by-section notes
* Local issues
* Missing information
* Ambiguous or unsupported claims

---

## 3. Claim Extraction and Evidence Mapping

**Goal:** Make the review grounded in explicit claims rather than vague impressions.

**Actions:**

* Extract all major claims made by the authors.
* Categorize claims into:

  * Novelty claims
  * Technical correctness claims
  * Empirical performance claims
  * Theoretical claims
  * Practical impact claims
  * Generalization claims
* For each claim, identify the evidence provided in the paper.
* Mark whether the evidence is strong, weak, missing, or irrelevant.

**Outputs:**

* Claim-evidence table
* Unsupported claims
* Overstated claims
* Claims requiring external verification

---

## 4. Novelty Check

**Goal:** Judge whether the work is genuinely new compared with prior work.

### 4.1 External Similarity Search

**Actions:**

* Search the web, Google Scholar, Semantic Scholar, arXiv, OpenReview, Papers With Code, and venue proceedings.
* Retrieve similar papers by:

  * Method
  * Problem setting
  * Dataset
  * Architecture
  * Theoretical framework
  * Evaluation protocol
* Compare the current paper against the most relevant prior work.

**Outputs:**

* List of similar papers
* Similarity/difference analysis
* Potential missing citations
* Novelty risk level: low / medium / high

### 4.2 Contribution Differentiation

**Questions:**

* What exactly is new?
* Is the novelty conceptual, technical, empirical, theoretical, or application-driven?
* Is the difference from prior work substantial or incremental?
* Does the paper clearly position itself against the closest prior work?

**Outputs:**

* Novelty assessment
* Missing related work
* Incrementality judgment

---

## 5. Significance and Impact Analysis

**Goal:** Evaluate whether the contribution matters to the field.

**Actions:**

* Use multiple specialized agents to discuss significance:

  * Domain expert agent
  * Methodology expert agent
  * Empirical evaluation agent
  * Skeptical reviewer agent
  * Practical impact agent
* Each agent independently answers:

  * What does this work add to the field?
  * Who would benefit from it?
  * Does it solve an important problem?
  * Is the improvement meaningful?
  * Could the idea influence future work?

**Outputs:**

* Multi-agent significance discussion
* Consensus and disagreement points
* Significance score
* Impact assessment

---

## 6. Rigor Check

**Goal:** Evaluate whether the paper is technically and scientifically sound.

### 6.1 Internal Correctness

**Actions:**

* Check definitions, assumptions, derivations, algorithms, and experimental procedures.
* Verify whether theoretical arguments follow logically.
* Check whether the method description is complete enough to reproduce.
* Identify hidden assumptions or possible failure cases.

**Outputs:**

* Technical correctness notes
* Ambiguous or invalid reasoning
* Reproducibility concerns

### 6.2 Claim Support Check

**Actions:**

* Revisit the claim-evidence map.
* Check whether each major claim is supported by sufficient evidence.
* Distinguish between demonstrated results and speculative conclusions.
* Flag cases where experiments do not support the stated claims.

**Outputs:**

* Supported claims
* Partially supported claims
* Unsupported or overstated claims

### 6.3 Experimental Rigor

**Questions:**

* Are the baselines appropriate and strong?
* Are datasets suitable and sufficiently diverse?
* Are metrics appropriate?
* Are ablations sufficient?
* Are statistical significance or uncertainty estimates provided?
* Are hyperparameters and implementation details clear?
* Are negative results or limitations discussed?

**Outputs:**

* Experiment quality assessment
* Missing baselines
* Missing ablations
* Possible confounders
* Reproducibility score

---

## 7. Review Planning

**Goal:** Convert analysis into a coherent reviewer perspective.

**Actions:**

* Summarize the main strengths.
* Summarize the main weaknesses.
* Identify fatal flaws versus minor issues.
* Decide recommendation tendency:

  * Strong accept
  * Accept
  * Weak accept
  * Borderline
  * Weak reject
  * Reject
  * Strong reject
* Decide confidence level.

**Outputs:**

* Strengths list
* Weaknesses list
* Questions for authors
* Recommendation rationale
* Confidence estimate

---

## 8. Draft Review

**Goal:** Generate a structured review for the authors.

**Recommended structure:**

1. Summary of the paper
2. Main strengths
3. Main weaknesses
4. Detailed comments
5. Questions for authors
6. Suggestions for improvement
7. Overall recommendation
8. Reviewer confidence

**Style requirements:**

* Be specific and evidence-based.
* Avoid vague criticism.
* Separate factual issues from subjective judgments.
* Be constructive and respectful.
* Mention both positive and negative aspects.

**Outputs:**

* Full draft review
* Optional short meta-review
* Optional score card

---

## 9. Self-Critique and Review Verification

**Goal:** Check whether the generated review is accurate, fair, and grounded.

**Actions:**

* Verify every criticism against the paper.
* Check whether the review misrepresents the authors' claims.
* Check whether the review missed important strengths or weaknesses.
* Check whether the recommendation is consistent with the evidence.
* Check whether the tone is professional.
* Check whether external novelty claims are supported by reliable sources.

**Questions:**

* Are any comments hallucinated?
* Are any claims too strong?
* Are all major criticisms actionable?
* Is the review balanced?
* Would another reviewer find the reasoning convincing?

**Outputs:**

* Self-critique report
* List of corrections
* Revised review draft

---

## 10. Finalization

**Goal:** Produce the final review after correction.

**Actions:**

* Incorporate self-critique feedback.
* Remove unsupported criticism.
* Clarify vague comments.
* Strengthen evidence-based reasoning.
* Ensure consistency between summary, weaknesses, score, and recommendation.

**Final outputs:**

* Final review
* Recommendation
* Confidence score
* Optional structured JSON report
* Optional author-facing improvement checklist
